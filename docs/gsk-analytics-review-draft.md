## Draft

**Role:** Lead Product Designer / sole UX lead on the project  
**Timeline:** 2020-2021  
**Scope:** UX strategy, product design, usability testing, stakeholder management, platform advocacy, analyst feedback loops

At GSK, I led the redesign of AIM (Analytics and Insights for Medical), a global analytics platform used by Medical Insights Analysts to identify trends across large, complex datasets. The goal was straightforward: turn a failing tool into a system analysts could actually use to explore data, generate insights, and support decision-making.

The product outcome was strong. The environment around it was not.

This project ultimately produced some of the clearest before-and-after gains of my design career: usability rose from a SUS score of 34 to 81, load times dropped from more than 30 minutes to seconds, and analyst engagement increased substantially. It also exposed, in a lasting way, how difficult it is for design to succeed in an organization that lacks the conditions to support it.

## The challenge

For more than a decade, GSK had collected extensive field data from physicians, nurses, and patients covering side effects, prescribing patterns, and competitive insights. Despite the richness of that information, there was no effective way to analyze or synthesize it at scale. AIM was intended to solve that problem, but the initial implementation, built on Power BI, failed almost immediately.

The platform could not handle the scale or complexity of the data, load times routinely exceeded 30 minutes, and the visualizations were too limited for the exploratory work analysts actually needed to do. After six months in production, usage was low and trust in the product was eroding.

Technically, the problem was obvious. Organizationally, it was much harder.

I was the lead and only UX voice on the project, working in an environment with low UX maturity, weak product collaboration, and very little shared understanding of how design should operate. The Product Owner had previously acted as both PO and designer and was reluctant to let go of design authority. Decisions were frequently undermined, collaboration was poor, and much of the work required not only solving the product problem itself, but navigating a structure that made it difficult to align on reality.

As the project continued, the environment deteriorated further. Leadership frustration over the platform's performance often turned into blame instead of clarity. Meetings became places where product, design, engineering, and leadership deflected accountability rather than solving the underlying issue. At the same time, the organization introduced reductive "designer maturity" evaluations that attempted to measure individual worth without addressing the structural problems making good work harder than it needed to be.

## Diagnosing the real problem

Initial usability testing produced a SUS score of 34, confirming that the dashboard was failing its users. Iterative improvements within Power BI raised the score only to 51, but that was as far as the platform would take us. The conclusion was clear: this was not a polish problem. It was a platform problem.

I spent months advocating for that conclusion in an environment that repeatedly required proof for what should have been obvious from the user experience itself. The burden was not just designing a better product. It was continually making the case that the existing one was fundamentally incapable of succeeding.

## Rebuilding the platform

Working with the Product Owner, I spent eight to nine months pushing for a full rebuild using a custom technology stack centered on Plotly. That shift provided the flexibility, performance, and control needed for real analytical work.

The redesign focused on four things:

- dramatically faster performance
- navigation and workflows aligned to actual analyst behavior
- visualizations tailored to medical insight discovery
- a clearer visual language aligned with GSK's design system

At the same time, analyst trust had to be rebuilt. To keep users engaged during the transition, I introduced a weekly "You Said, We Listened" email series to show how feedback was shaping the product in real time. We also used live demos during every focus group to create transparency, reinforce accountability, and show that the team was responding to what analysts were telling us.

## Results and impact

- SUS improved from 34 to 81 across the redesign
- Iterations within Power BI raised SUS only to 51; the rebuild was what unlocked real usability gains
- Load times dropped from 30+ minutes to seconds
- Insight generation became roughly 3x faster
- Adoption reached 85%
- Analyst trust improved through visible feedback loops and regular demo-based validation
- The work received GSK's Gold Award in May 2021, the company's highest internal recognition

## Reflection

This project changed the course of my career.

On paper, it was a major success. The product improved dramatically, analysts could finally use the platform as intended, and the work was formally recognized at the highest level inside the company. But the effort required to get there was far beyond what should have been necessary.

I was operating as the sole UX voice in a system with low design maturity, weak trust, poor collaboration, and very little structural support for design to do its job well. Instead of building on shared understanding, I spent enormous energy proving obvious truths, protecting decisions, managing stakeholder dysfunction, and pushing through an environment that treated symptoms as politics instead of solving causes.

That experience taught me something I have carried ever since: good product outcomes are not enough on their own. If the organization lacks the conditions for design to succeed, even strong results can come at an unsustainable human cost.

I am proud of the work. I earned the recognition it received. But GSK was also the experience that broke my passion for design for a long time and pushed me toward Design Operations, where I could focus on building the structures that make strong product work more sustainable in the first place.
