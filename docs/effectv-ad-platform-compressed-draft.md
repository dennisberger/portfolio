## Draft

**Role:** Lead Product Designer with front-end implementation ownership  
**Timeline:** 0-to-1 launch phase

At Comcast Advertising, I led design for the launch of the first self-service linear TV ad buying platform in the U.S., created for small and "super-small" businesses that had historically viewed TV advertising as expensive, opaque, and out of reach.

The challenge was not just to digitize a media-buying workflow. It was to translate a complex, high-touch process into something ordinary business owners could understand, trust, and complete on their own.

## What I changed

### 1. Rebuilt the workflow around self-service

I designed the experience end to end across onboarding, campaign setup, ad planning, dashboards, and browser-based commercial creation. The product was built as a progressive web app, but the real goal was to simplify the process itself, not just port it to a new interface.

### 2. Treated language as a UX lever

Usability testing revealed that jargon was one of the biggest barriers to adoption. Terms like "spots," "inventory," and "linear opportunities" created hesitation because users understood their businesses, not the advertising industry.

I rewrote the experience in plainer, more supportive language and added contextual explanations that made the workflow easier to follow and trust.

### 3. Tested and validated the simplification

I used iterative usability testing to measure whether the experience was actually becoming easier to use. Those rounds showed meaningful improvements in both efficiency and confidence, validating that the design and language shifts were working.

## Results and impact

- Improved SUS from 41 to 72 across testing rounds
- Reduced clicks per task by more than 40%
- Cut task completion time by roughly 50% in usability testing
- Shifted user feedback from confusion and hesitation to clarity and confidence

## Reflection

This project reinforced a lesson that has shaped much of my work since: clarity is a product feature.

Self-service only works when the product speaks in the user's language, not the industry's. As a former small business owner myself, this project was especially meaningful because it turned something intimidating into something possible.
