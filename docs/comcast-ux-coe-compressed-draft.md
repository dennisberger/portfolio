## Draft

**Role:** Senior Program Manager for Digital Experience  
**Timeline:** 2021 onward during the ULearn transformation

During the Great Resignation, Comcast reimagined its employee learning ecosystem as ULearn, a modernized experience built to support compliance, management development, and field enablement. The instructional design capability behind the work was strong, but the new organization lacked UX maturity, user-centered methods, and a clear way to operationalize UX inside a delivery environment already under pressure.

I was brought in to help structure and operationalize that new capability. Over time, that work evolved into the creation of a recognized UX Center of Excellence: a team that could both deliver UX work directly and teach the broader organization how to apply UX thinking in its own processes.

## The challenge

ULearn was replacing a legacy SAP-based employee learning experience that many employees ignored altogether. Completion rates on started content were below 20%, and the new UX function entered an environment where stakeholders were skeptical of a discipline they did not yet understand.

The team itself was also uneven in experience. At one point it consisted of six designers, including two senior practitioners and four much more junior designers converted from Learning Experience Design roles. That meant UX had to prove its value while also becoming more operationally sustainable.

## What I changed

### 1. Used measurement to make UX credible

I introduced SUS as a way to separate usability problems from completion metrics and give skeptical stakeholders a more objective lens on learning quality. Average scores on untouched products typically landed in the 40s to 50s, while products we directly iterated on could reach 68+ within two to three sprint cycles.

That measurement strategy helped reposition UX from a subjective or aesthetic concern into a practical lever for improving learning effectiveness.

### 2. Structured the function as a Center of Excellence

As the team matured, we realized the function worked best as a Center of Excellence rather than a conventional embedded team. I helped define that model so UX could both deliver work directly and enable other teams to adopt user-centered methods themselves.

That framing gave the organization a clearer understanding of what UX was for, how it partnered with the business, and how it could scale its influence beyond a small central team.

### 3. Reduced UX drag with a tiered service model

Early on, bringing UX into a project could add a full sprint to delivery timelines. I helped address that by introducing a tiered engagement model that matched the level of UX involvement to the needs of the work and the seniority of the designer assigned to it.

Alongside that shift, we brought in stronger external talent, invested in upskilling for more junior team members, and created facilitation and coaching pathways that let teams solve lower-complexity UX issues without always requiring full redesign engagement.

Over six months, the added timeline burden of UX dropped from roughly one full sprint to about two to three days, with a 33% reduction in UX drag from peak.

### 4. Expanded UX capability beyond the core team

I helped build enablement practices that extended UX thinking into instructional design and adjacent teams, including upskilling approaches, shared frameworks, and design thinking facilitation. I also supported the team's move from Adobe XD to Figma to improve collaboration as the function matured.

## Results and impact

- Helped establish Comcast ULearn's UX function as a recognized Center of Excellence
- Introduced usability measurement into the organization through SUS baselines and iterative testing
- Improved touched products from typical SUS baselines in the 40s-50s to 68+ within two to three sprint cycles
- Reduced UX drag from roughly one sprint to two to three days over six months
- Reduced UX drag by 33% from peak while the function continued to grow
- Structured a six-person team with mixed seniority into a more sustainable operating model
- Expanded UX capability beyond the core team through coaching, facilitation, and shared methods
- Supported the team's migration from Adobe XD to Figma

## Reflection

This work reinforced that early-stage UX functions in enterprise environments do not earn trust through conviction alone. They earn it by becoming measurable, understandable, and sustainable.

At Comcast ULearn, my contribution was to help make UX legible to the business: first through evidence, then through service design, and finally through enablement that let the function scale beyond the boundaries of the team itself.
