## Draft

**Role:** Senior Program Manager for Digital Experience  
**Timeline:** 2021 onward during the ULearn transformation  
**Scope:** UX operating model, team structuring, measurement strategy, stakeholder education, service design, tool migration, cross-functional enablement

During the Great Resignation, many enterprise organizations were investing more heavily in employee training, upskilling, and career development as a way to improve retention and quality of life. Comcast had an opportunity to do the same, but its existing employee learning experience was built on legacy SAP software and widely ignored by employees who often chose outside learning options instead.

That moment led to the creation of ULearn, a reimagined employee learning organization structured around compliance, management and promotion training, and field enablement for technicians, installers, and retail employees. The instructional design capability behind the work was strong. What the organization lacked was user experience thinking, UX methods, and a clear way to operationalize UX inside a delivery environment that was already under pressure.

I was brought in as Senior Program Manager for Digital Experience to help structure and operationalize this new UX function. Over time, that work evolved into the creation of a recognized UX Center of Excellence: a team that could both deliver UX work directly and teach the broader organization how to incorporate user-centered methods into product and learning design decisions.

## The challenge

The new UX function entered an environment with low UX awareness, low UX maturity, and limited patience for anything that slowed delivery. Stakeholders in ULearn's three pillars were focused on shipping learning products tied to major enterprise priorities, not adopting a new discipline they did not yet fully understand.

The team itself was also uneven in experience. Comcast initially staffed the function with a mix of talent, including several people converted from Learning Experience Design into UX roles. That gave the organization additional hands, but not always the UX depth required to work confidently in a skeptical enterprise setting. At one point, the team consisted of six designers: two senior and four much more junior practitioners still building their UX foundations.

This created a credibility problem on two fronts. First, stakeholders needed proof that UX would improve learning experiences. Second, the UX team needed a way to contribute without becoming a bottleneck.

## Winning credibility through measurement

The initial business problem was clear: less than 20% of employees completed the ULearn content they started, including some required learning. But completion rate alone was not enough to make the case for UX. Stakeholders could treat low completion as a content or motivation problem. I needed a way to show that usability itself was part of the issue.

My approach was to introduce a measurable UX lens into the conversation. I pushed to use SUS as a way to decouple user experience quality from completion metrics and create a more objective baseline that skeptical stakeholders could understand. Average SUS scores on untouched products tended to land in the 40s to 50s, signaling experiences that were usable enough to function, but still well below what employees should expect from a modern learning platform.

That measurement strategy changed the conversation. Once stakeholders could see low completion paired with weak usability scores, UX became easier to defend as a business lever rather than an aesthetic preference. On products the team directly touched, we were able to push SUS above the industry average of 68 within two to three sprint cycles of focused iteration and testing.

That shift gave the function its opening. Where skepticism would not embrace the art of UX, it could respond to the science.

## Turning a fragile UX team into a Center of Excellence

As the function matured, we recognized that it was operating less like a traditional embedded design team and more like a Center of Excellence. Different parts of ULearn needed different levels of support. Some needed deep UX involvement. Others needed lightweight guidance, design thinking facilitation, or help learning how to apply UX methods themselves.

I helped structure the function around that reality. We defined the UX Center of Excellence as both an internal delivery partner and an enablement engine. In practice, that meant we could take on UX work directly where needed, or "teach teams to fish" so they could build stronger user-centered practices within their own workflows.

This CoE framing became the foundation for how UX partnered with the business, how capacity was managed, and how maturity would scale beyond the immediate design team.

## Reducing UX drag with a tiered engagement model

One of the biggest risks to the new function was that UX initially added too much drag to delivery. Early on, bringing UX into a learning product could add a full sprint to the timeline. Some of that was because the team was still learning on the job. Some of it came from a low-maturity tendency to frame too many problems as "rethink everything" opportunities, even when lighter interventions would have been more appropriate.

To make UX sustainable, I helped redesign how the team engaged with work. I introduced a tiered service model that matched the level of UX involvement to the needs of the project and the seniority of the designer assigned to it.

- Tier 1 supported high-impact strategic work where deeper UX involvement was justified
- Tier 2 focused on targeted consulting, review, and structured feedback
- Tier 3 emphasized lightweight guidance, enablement, and coaching that other teams could apply more independently

At the same time, we strengthened the team by bringing in more senior external UX talent and giving the less experienced designers structured upskilling, including Nielsen Norman training. I also helped establish a UX upskilling pillar and a design thinking facilitation practice so the function could solve problems not only through direct execution, but also through education and workshop-based support.

Over the course of six months, that model significantly reduced the operational burden of UX. What had started as roughly one extra sprint of timeline impact dropped to about a week within one quarter, then to roughly two to three days in the following quarter. At that point, the organization broadly agreed that the added time was reasonable given the value UX was bringing. Overall, UX drag was reduced by 33% from its peak even as the function continued to grow.

## Building capability beyond the design team

The longer-term goal was never just to make a six-person UX team more effective. It was to broaden UX literacy across ULearn so that better user-centered decisions could happen earlier and more often.

To support that, I helped create enablement approaches that extended beyond the core design team. This included building shared frameworks, teaching stakeholders how to interpret UX evidence, and helping instructional design and product teams incorporate user-centered thinking into their own planning and decision-making. The function became not only a service provider, but a multiplier.

I also supported the team's transition from Adobe XD to Figma, which improved collaboration and brought the design practice onto a more modern platform as UX operations matured.

## Results and impact

- Helped establish Comcast ULearn's UX function as a recognized Center of Excellence
- Introduced SUS as one of the organization's first usability benchmarks, shifting stakeholder conversations from opinion to evidence
- Demonstrated that products with direct UX iteration could improve from SUS baselines in the 40s-50s to 68+ within two to three sprint cycles
- Reduced UX drag from roughly one full sprint to two to three days over a six-month period
- Reduced UX drag by 33% from peak while the function continued to scale
- Structured a six-person team with mixed seniority into a more sustainable operating model
- Expanded UX capability beyond the immediate team through coaching, facilitation, and cross-functional enablement
- Led the team's migration from Adobe XD to Figma to support more collaborative, mature workflows

## Reflection

This work taught me that early-stage UX functions in enterprise environments do not survive on conviction alone. They survive by becoming legible to the business.

At Comcast ULearn, that meant proving value through measurement, designing a service model that matched maturity and capacity, and creating just enough structure for UX to scale without overwhelming delivery. The result was not just better learning experiences. It was a UX function that became credible, teachable, and sustainable inside a high-priority enterprise transformation.
