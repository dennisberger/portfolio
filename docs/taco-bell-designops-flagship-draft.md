## Recommended title

**Scaling Design Infrastructure at Taco Bell**

## Draft

**Role:** Design Operations Lead  
**Timeline:** December 2023 - Present  
**Scope:** Design operations, research operations, tooling, intake and planning, budget ownership, design system enablement, content strategy advocacy

When I joined Taco Bell in December 2023 as its first Design Operations Lead, the company was in the middle of bringing design in-house after years of relying on an external agency model. Design work was getting done, but the function was still operating like a reactive service team. Requests arrived informally, often through Slack. There was no consistent intake path, no shared view of capacity, no standard planning model, no research practice, and no real design system infrastructure to support scale.

My mandate was to help build the operating foundation that would allow Taco Bell's internal design organization to function as a durable enterprise capability rather than an order-taking layer. That meant creating the systems, tooling, rituals, and governance needed for design to plan work, defend decisions, collaborate effectively, and scale across app, web, and broader customer experience initiatives.

## The challenge

At the time, Taco Bell's internal design organization was still small: one design director, two internal designers, and me, alongside an agency team that had historically fulfilled requests from marketing and product with limited strategic pushback. That structure had created a delivery culture where design was expected to respond quickly, but not necessarily operate with strong standards, evidence, or long-term coordination.

Operationally, the team was fragmented:

- Stakeholders treated Slack as an intake channel for design work
- There was no reliable way to understand capacity, timelines, or delivery risk
- Design was working in Asana while much of the company was already operating in Jira
- Documentation lived in inconsistent places, including Figma, instead of in a shared knowledge base
- Figma was on a lower-tier plan, limiting governance and collaboration features such as branching and merging
- The "design system" was effectively a PowerPoint of components used as a loose reference, not a scalable system
- Design debates were driven by opinion and best practices because there was no dedicated research capability

For Taco Bell to truly own design as an internal function, all of that had to change.

## Building delivery discipline

One of my first priorities was establishing a real intake and planning model. I moved the team away from ad hoc request handling and into Jira, even though the transition was not initially popular. The goal was not process for process's sake. It was to make work visible, measurable, and governable.

I introduced a Jira-based operating model with structured intake, sprint planning, Fibonacci estimation, and sprint-level reporting. That gave the team a single source of truth for requested work, work in progress, completed work, and delivery risk. It also gave design a way to have more productive conversations with stakeholders about timelines, tradeoffs, and capacity constraints.

The behavioral shift was significant. Slack stopped functioning as an intake mechanism. If a request came in through chat, I redirected it into the formal workflow so the team could evaluate and prioritize it appropriately. That change moved design from reactive request fulfillment to a more disciplined planning model aligned with how the broader organization already worked.

## Building enterprise-grade design infrastructure

I also had to modernize the infrastructure supporting the team. Design was operating with tools and documentation practices that could not support scale or cross-functional alignment. I led the shift into a more enterprise-ready environment by moving core planning into Jira, centralizing documentation in Confluence, and securing leadership approval to upgrade from Figma Pro to Figma Enterprise.

That Figma migration was especially important. It gave the team access to the collaboration and governance features needed to support serious design operations, including branching and merging, and allowed us to introduce Dev Mode while retiring an outdated Zeplin setup that no longer met our needs. Taco Bell became the first team within Yum Brands to make that move, and the work later positioned me to support Yum's broader transition into a converged Figma Enterprise environment.

This part of the job was not just tool administration. It was infrastructure strategy. I was making decisions about how the organization would work, how handoff would improve, and what systems were necessary to support future growth.

## Making research operational

The biggest capability gap I identified early was research. Without user evidence, design and product conversations kept collapsing into subjective debates. Teams were relying on opinion, instinct, and generic best practices rather than learning from actual Taco Bell customers. The result was visible in the product itself.

I made the case for bringing research into the organization and led the process to establish it through an external partner. I owned the statement of work negotiation, set up the operational model, and became the internal point of contact responsible for ensuring the research team could map their workflows to Taco Bell's product and stakeholder realities.

Over time, I built the operating layer around that partnership:

- research intake and triage
- capacity management
- Jira workflow design
- recurring planning rituals
- internal advocacy and adoption
- stakeholder communication through outreach like a periodic research newsletter

That work changed how design decisions were made. Before this, Taco Bell was effectively doing zero formal studies. Now research is embedded in major product work, including the current full app redesign. The team has run industry analysis, unmoderated testing on early concepts, and moderated testing on near-final designs, making it possible to refine the experience based on evidence instead of opinion.

The capability also scaled quickly. Taco Bell went from zero formal studies in 2023 to 22 studies in 2024 and 64 studies in 2025. Research headcount was introduced in August 2024 and expanded by 33% in January 2026, growing to a team of four. I continue to directly manage the relationship and operations, while partnering closely with the vendor's program lead to keep the practice efficient, productive, and aligned to rising demand across product, marketing, and in-restaurant work.

## Enabling a real design system practice

I did not design Taco Bell's component system myself. My role was to create the operating conditions that would allow a real design system practice to emerge and eventually stabilize.

Early on, there was no true system, only disconnected artifacts that were insufficient for a growing in-house team. I helped establish the initial structure for design system work by creating a dedicated Jira board, defining a sprint framework, introducing lightweight capacity estimation, and helping the team frame progress and constraints in ways leadership could understand.

As the system matured, I continued to support it through the operational layer: tooling, intake, governance, triage, office hours, documentation strategy, and collaboration workflows. I sourced Zeroheight to support documentation, helped implement the Figma Enterprise and Dev Mode capabilities the team needed, and created the visibility mechanisms required to show momentum, highlight constraints, and support more realistic planning. The system has since grown to more than 60 components supporting Taco Bell's evolving digital experiences.

Today I continue to lead the Taco Bell design system team's operating model, including an early pilot using ChatGPT and Figma MCP to identify token and component inconsistencies in read-only mode. Together with Taco Bell's earlier move to Figma Enterprise, this represents two enterprise-wide firsts: the first Yum brand to move from Figma Pro/Org to Figma Enterprise, and the first team piloting AI-assisted design system governance patterns intended to scale more broadly across Yum over time.

## Designing the organization, not just the workflow

As the function grew, my scope expanded beyond process and tooling. I became deeply involved in shaping the broader operating model for the organization, including staffing inputs, contractor planning, budget ownership, and capability development.

I own a $2.3M annual operating budget covering tooling, vendor relationships, contractor support, and research infrastructure. I also co-manage, with Taco Bell's Director of Engineering, the relationship with Applause, an external accessibility validation partner supporting quality and compliance across the product experience. I have supported annual planning, hiring discussions, interviewing, and capacity analysis to help translate business needs into staffing decisions. Since I joined, the internal design organization has grown from two internal designers to eight full-time designers plus four contractors, alongside a four-person research team.

I also identified content strategy as another missing capability. Designers were writing product copy themselves, often while trying to interpret brand voice, legal constraints, and UX needs at the same time. I advocated for a dedicated Content Strategy Lead headcount in late 2024, and that role was eventually hired in October 2025. The addition gave the team a true brand and product language partner, improved quality across the app experience, and likely reduced design cycle time by roughly half a sprint by allowing designers to focus more fully on UX and UI decisions.

## Results and impact

The most important outcome of this work is that Taco Bell's design organization now operates with infrastructure it did not previously have.

- Design intake no longer happens through Slack; work is routed through Jira with clear visibility and planning discipline
- The organization has a single source of truth for work, progress, and delivery risk
- Taco Bell established two enterprise-wide firsts: becoming the first Yum brand to move to Figma Enterprise and the first team piloting AI-assisted design system governance with ChatGPT and Figma MCP
- Research went from nonexistent to an embedded capability supporting product, marketing, and in-restaurant decision-making
- Formal studies scaled from 0 in 2023 to 22 in 2024 and 64 in 2025
- Research capacity grew by 33% in January 2026 to meet increasing demand
- A real design system operating model now exists, supported by tooling, governance, documentation, and triage workflows, with more than 60 components now in the system
- A dedicated content strategy function is now part of the team, reducing copy burden on designers and improving focus
- The internal function scaled from a small in-house team with agency dependence to a substantially larger organization with stronger internal capability

Just as importantly, design is now better equipped to challenge assumptions, justify decisions, and work as a strategic partner rather than a reactive service layer.

## Reflection

This work taught me that Design Operations is not just about process efficiency. In enterprise environments, it is the mechanism that turns design from a set of individual contributors into a function the business can trust.

At Taco Bell, my job was not to make process heavier. It was to build the conditions that allowed design to become more accountable, evidence-based, and scalable. That meant doing what was necessary to keep the function moving through change, create clarity where there was ambiguity, and give teams the operational support they needed to do better work.

The result is not just a cleaner workflow. It is a stronger design organization with more leverage, more credibility, and a much better foundation for the work still ahead.
